018260 Business Transformation · Assessment 01

AI-driven disruption in retail

A structured literature review examining how artificial intelligence is reshaping retail operations, customer experience and business-model innovation.

Explore project +

Task & objective

Synthesise current peer-reviewed research to identify the principal ways AI disrupts retail value creation and evaluate the strategic implications, risks and research gaps.

My approach

I searched Scopus, Web of Science, ScienceDirect, Taylor & Francis and Google Scholar using defined keywords and inclusion criteria. I compared studies published from 2020–2025 and organised the evidence into three themes: operational transformation, customer experience and business-model innovation.

Core findings & outcome

AI improves forecasting, inventory decisions, personalisation and vendor-risk evaluation, but benefits depend on data quality, governance and organisational capability. The review also identified unresolved concerns around privacy, bias, implementation cost and limited long-term empirical evidence. I completed a 2,124-word individual review with a comparative evidence table and recommendations for responsible adoption.

028260 Business Transformation · Assessment 02

Sky TV digital transformation strategy

An evidence-based strategic plan for repositioning Sky Network Television from a legacy satellite broadcaster to a competitive digital-first platform.

Explore project +

Task & objective

Develop a feasible response to global streaming competition, changing viewing behaviour and the cost and scalability limits of Sky’s legacy infrastructure.

My approach

I combined disruption theory, business-model analysis and SWOT with dynamic-capabilities thinking. I defined a future vision and SMART objectives, then designed strategic initiatives, a phased implementation roadmap, change communication and governance KPIs.

Core findings & outcome

The 2,660-word individual plan recommended a cloud-based OTT platform, cross-functional digital teams, phased cost reallocation from satellite infrastructure and data-led customer engagement. It translated the analysis into measurable subscriber, retention, platform-delivery and cost targets, while treating the recommendations as a proposed strategy rather than claiming completed implementation.

038271 Māori Business Leadership Models · Assessment 02

Global expansion through Māori leadership

A case analysis showing how Te Kete Aronui could pursue international growth while protecting employee relationships, ethical legitimacy and cultural values.

Explore project +

Task & objective

Diagnose falling morale, leadership–staff disconnection and ethical supply-chain risk arising during rapid international expansion, then propose culturally grounded solutions.

My approach

I analysed the case through whanaungatanga, kaitiakitanga and rangatiratanga, connecting Māori leadership scholarship with employee engagement, stakeholder theory, ethical sourcing and long-term strategic sustainability.

Core findings & outcome

The 2,585-word individual analysis found that the operational problems were fundamentally issues of leadership and values alignment. I proposed recurring employee–management hui to rebuild participation and trust, plus a Kaitiakitanga Ethical Supply Chain Assurance Framework using independent oversight, supplier standards and transparent reporting. The project showed that Māori values can strengthen, rather than restrict, sustainable global growth.

04 / Reflective learning

Learning journal

Experience becomes useful when we pause to understand it.

01

Learning to lead through listening

Leadership & teamwork · 228 words
+

One of the most valuable lessons from my ICL study has been that leadership begins with listening, not with having the loudest voice. During a group assignment, our team initially struggled to agree on priorities. I focused too quickly on solutions and did not give enough space to quieter members. Once I began asking open questions and summarising each person’s viewpoint, the conversation changed. We found that the disagreement was not about the final goal, but about how we understood the task and divided responsibility.

This learning has been useful because it changed the way I participate in academic teamwork. I now prepare for meetings by identifying the decisions we need to make, while remaining flexible about how we reach them. Active listening has helped me communicate more clearly, recognise different working styles and create a safer environment for people to contribute. It has also improved my self-awareness: leadership is not only about directing work; it is about helping the group use everyone’s strengths.

I will apply this in professional settings through clear expectations, regular check-ins and shared decision-making. When conflict occurs, I will separate the person from the problem and ask what information or need may be missing. My aim is to become a calm, inclusive leader who builds trust and helps a team move forward together.

02

Turning complexity into a clear plan

Project management · 219 words
+

A project management exercise taught me how important it is to turn a broad objective into smaller, measurable pieces of work. At first, our assignment felt difficult because the scope was wide and the deadline seemed close. I learned to define the intended outcome, identify stakeholders, break the work into tasks and place those tasks on a realistic timeline. A simple responsibility matrix also helped us see who owned each activity and where collaboration was required.

This skill was useful immediately. Instead of reacting to the most urgent message, our group could work from an agreed plan and review progress against milestones. I also learned that a plan should not be treated as fixed. When one research task took longer than expected, we adjusted the sequence without losing sight of the final objective. This reduced stress and improved the consistency of our final submission.

In a real-world context, I will apply this approach by clarifying scope before committing resources, setting success measures early and communicating risks before they become problems. I also want to improve my use of digital project tools and post-project reviews. The experience showed me that good project management is not simply making a schedule; it is creating shared clarity, anticipating uncertainty and helping people make confident decisions throughout the work.

03

Using evidence to make better decisions

Business analysis · 216 words
+

Through business analysis coursework, I learned to distinguish an interesting observation from a decision supported by evidence. In one task, I reviewed market information and initially focused on figures that confirmed my first idea. After comparing sources and questioning assumptions, I found customer behaviour was more complex than expected. The strongest recommendation was not the most ambitious option, but the one that best matched the evidence and organisational capability.

This learning improved my academic work because I now ask three questions: where did the information come from, what might be missing, and how does it support the recommendation? It has made my reports more balanced and helped me explain limitations instead of hiding them. I also became more comfortable changing my view when new information appears, which I see as a professional strength rather than indecision.

I plan to apply this habit in management roles by combining quantitative information with stakeholder perspectives. Before recommending action, I will define decision criteria, test alternative explanations and present benefits and risks. In practical terms, this means using concise dashboards, documenting assumptions and making reasoning visible to others. Evidence does not remove uncertainty, but it creates a stronger foundation for responsible decisions and makes it easier for a team to learn from the outcome.

04

Communicating across cultures

Intercultural communication · 218 words
+

Studying in a diverse classroom has shown me that effective communication is not only about speaking accurate English. People may have different expectations about directness, silence, disagreement and participation. During a class discussion, I noticed that a message I intended as efficient could sound too direct without context. I learned to pause, check understanding and adapt my language to the person and situation rather than assuming one communication style works for everyone.

This has been especially useful in group work. I now explain the purpose behind a request, invite questions and confirm decisions in writing. These small actions reduce misunderstanding and make collaboration more inclusive. The experience also helped me value cultural difference as a resource: people notice different risks, interpret customers differently and contribute ideas shaped by their own experience.

In my professional life in New Zealand, I will develop cultural intelligence by observing carefully, asking respectful questions and reflecting on feedback. I want to learn more about Te Tiriti o Waitangi and values that shape responsible work in Aotearoa. With international teams, I will agree on communication norms early and create more than one way for people to contribute. My goal is not to remove difference, but to build enough trust and clarity that difference becomes a source of better thinking.

05

Presenting ideas with purpose

Professional communication · 212 words
+

A presentation assessment helped me understand that a professional presentation should be designed around the audience, not around everything the speaker knows. My early slides contained too much detail because I was trying to prove I had completed the research. Through rehearsal and feedback, I learned to organise the presentation around one clear message, select only the evidence that supported it and use visual material to guide attention rather than compete with my words.

This learning was useful because it improved both my confidence and the audience’s understanding. Rehearsal allowed me to replace memorised sentences with key ideas, maintain better eye contact and respond more naturally to questions. I also learned to signpost the structure so listeners understood where the discussion was going and why the recommendation mattered.

I will apply this skill whenever I brief colleagues, propose a project or speak with clients. Before creating slides, I will identify what the audience already knows, what decision they need to make and what action I want them to take. I will keep supporting detail available for questions instead of placing it all on screen. I want to become a communicator who makes complex information accessible without oversimplifying it, and who presents recommendations with clarity, evidence and respect for the audience’s time.

06

Peer feedback: making my contribution visible

Peer evaluation · 220 words
+

Feedback from classmates described me as reliable, thoughtful and well prepared. They appreciated that I completed assigned work on time and helped the group maintain quality. They also suggested I could communicate progress earlier and share unfinished ideas instead of waiting until the work felt complete. This was useful because I had viewed careful preparation as entirely positive, without recognising that silence during the process could make coordination difficult.

The feedback changed my understanding of teamwork. A strong contribution is not only the quality of the final task; it is also the visibility of progress, questions and risks. When I wait too long to share, the team loses the opportunity to offer input or adjust related work. I have started giving short updates during projects, explaining what is complete, what I am working on and where I need support. I also share early drafts with a clear note that they are open for discussion.

In future, I will use agreed check-in points and raise concerns as soon as they may affect the group. I will continue being dependable, while becoming more open and collaborative during development. The peer evaluation reminded me that feedback is most valuable when it leads to a specific behaviour change. My next step is to ask for feedback earlier, not only after work is finished.
